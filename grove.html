So What
Miles Davis · Kind of Blue · 1959
2:03 5:33
♩
Structure
Built on D Dorian mode — a scale that creates an open, floating feeling. No tension, no resolution. Just color.
♭
Harmony
Only two chords in the entire piece. Most jazz used 30+. Davis stripped it to pure space.
⟳
Tempo
At 136 BPM, the rhythm section walks with such confidence that soloists play across the beat, not with it.
∿
Dynamics
Never exceeds mezzo-forte. Restraint makes every gesture feel enormous. Volume as punctuation.
📅
Origin
Two sessions — March 2 and April 22, 1959. Davis gave musicians almost no written music. The spontaneity is the record.
🌍
Influence
Modal jazz opened the door to Coltrane's sheets of sound, ambient music, and everything in between. This is the exact pivot point.
👥
Ensemble
Coltrane, Adderley, Evans, Kelly, Chambers, Cobb. Six at their absolute peak, playing as one instrument.

An instrumental with a story

0:00 – 1:28  The theme. Weightless. An invitation into a room with no walls.

1:28 – 3:20  Miles takes the first solo. The space between notes is the message.

3:20 – 4:42  Coltrane enters. The temperature rises. Longer sentences, more urgency.

4:42 – 5:33  The return. Exactly where we started, yet we are changed for having been there.

🌊
Same depth
A Love Supreme — Coltrane. The modal universe pushed to its absolute spiritual limit. Four movements, one devotion.
🌿
Similar structure
Maiden Voyage — Hancock. Modal harmony as oceanic imagery. Liquid, drifting, luminous.
☀️
Unexpected bridge
Ze Meke — Mulatu Astatke. Ethiopian jazz. Same modal spaciousness, entirely different gravity.
